# Designated Hitter (DH)

The Designated Hitter rule allows teams to substitute a designated hitter for their pitcher in the batting order during each game, so the pitcher does not have to bat.

## History

- The **American League** adopted the DH rule in the **1970s**, becoming known for higher-scoring games
- The **National League** resisted the change for decades, maintaining a focus on pitching strategy and defense where pitchers batted for themselves
- The DH was the **last major rule difference** between the AL and NL
- In **2022**, **universal DH rules** were standardized across both leagues, eliminating the final significant rule discrepancy

(source: [al-nl-rule-differences.md](../sources/al-nl-rule-differences.md))

## Impact

The DH rule was a defining characteristic of the two leagues' identities:
- AL games tended to feature more offense
- NL games were considered more strategically complex due to managing the pitcher's spot in the lineup
- Universalizing the DH removed this distinction, simplifying the game for fans following both leagues
